## Final Answer: 
The **Past Simple** and the **Present Perfect** are both past tenses in English, but they are used in different situations. The key difference lies in their 
relationship to the present.

**Past Simple**

*   **Use:** The Past Simple is used for actions that started and finished in the past. The time of the action is usually stated or implied. It's a completed
action with no connection to the present.
*   **Time Frame:**  The time is definite, known, or understood.
*   **Keywords:** *yesterday, last week, last year, ago, in 2005, when I was a child, etc.*
*   **Structure:**  Subject + Verb (past form)

    *   Regular verbs: add -ed (e.g., *walked, played, watched*)
    *   Irregular verbs: have different forms (e.g., *went, saw, ate*)
*   **Examples:**
    *   "I **visited** Paris last summer." (Specific time: last summer)
    *   "She **watched** a movie yesterday." (Specific time: yesterday)
    *   "They **bought** a new car in 2020." (Specific time: 2020)
    *   "He **lived** in London for five years." (Finished action in the past. He doesn't live there now.)
    *   "When I was a child, I **played** soccer every day."

**Present Perfect**

*   **Use:** The Present Perfect is used for actions that started in the past and have a connection to the present. This connection can be:
    *   A past action that has a result now.
    *   An action that happened at an unspecified time in the past.
    *   An action that started in the past and is still continuing.
*   **Time Frame:** The time is indefinite or not important.  The focus is on the experience or result.
*   **Keywords:** *ever, never, already, yet, just, recently, since, for, so far, up to now, this week/month/year (when the period is not finished)*
*   **Structure:** Subject + have/has + Verb (past participle)

    *   Past participle of regular verbs: usually the same as the past form (-ed)
    *   Past participle of irregular verbs: varies (e.g., *gone, seen, eaten*)
*   **Examples:**
    *   "I **have visited** Paris." (Unspecified time. The experience of visiting Paris is important).
    *   "She **has broken** her leg." (The result is important: she can't walk now).
    *   "They **have lived** here since 2010." (The action started in the past and continues to the present).
    *   "He **has never eaten** sushi." (Experience up to now).
    *   "Have you **seen** that movie yet?" (Asking about an experience).
    *   "I **have just finished** my homework." (Action completed recently and relevant now).

**Here's a table summarizing the differences:**

| Feature          | Past Simple                                       | Present Perfect                                             |
|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Time**          | Specific, definite, finished                      | Unspecified, indefinite, connection to the present          |
| **Focus**         | Completed action in the past                      | Result or relevance of past action now                     |
| **Connection to Present** | No connection                               | Strong connection                                           |
| **Keywords**      | *yesterday, last week, ago, in [year]*          | *ever, never, yet, since, for, just, already, recently*   |
| **Example**        | I *saw* a movie yesterday.                     | I *have seen* that movie.                               |

**In short:**

*   Use the **Past Simple** when you are talking about a finished action at a specific time in the past.
*   Use the **Present Perfect** when you are talking about a past action that is connected to the present, either through its result, its relevance, or its 
continuation.
